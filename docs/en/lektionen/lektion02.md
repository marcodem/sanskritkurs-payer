# Lesson 2

## 2.1. The Nominal Sentence

Schema: Predicate Noun - Subject

e.g. devo viṣṇuḥ = देवो विष्णुः = "Viṣṇu is a god."

A verbal copula ("is", "are", "am", "are", "are") is not necessary, but can sometimes occur.

There are no articles: devaḥ -- देवः can mean "the god" or "a god".

Although in Sanskrit the word order is fairly free (especially in verses), one must always primarily consider a translation according to the above standard schema when translating a nominal sentence.

In a nominal sentence, the subject is in the nominative (first case = prathamā f. = प्रथमा). The predicate noun agrees with the subject in number and case; if the predicate noun is an adjective, also in gender.

## 2.2. On Declension (Inflection of Nouns)

In Sanskrit there are

*   three numbers (numeri) = vacana n. -- वचन 
    *   Singular = ekavacana n. -- एकवचन
    *   Dual = dvivacana n. -- द्विवचन
    *   Plural = bahuvacana n. -- बहुवचन 
*   three genders (genera) = liṅga n. / vyakti f. -- लिङ्ग / व्यक्ति 
    *   Masculine = puṃs m. -- पुंस्
    *   Feminine = strī f. -- स्त्री
    *   Neuter = napuṃsaka n. -- नपुंसक 
*   eight cases (kasus) = sup -- सुप् (= case endings)

For declension, the case endings (sup = सुप्) are added to the so-called nominal stem (the form of the noun without case endings). So for example devas = देवस् (Nominative Singular Masculine = first case singular masculine = prathamā ekavacanam puṃs = प्रथमा एकवचनम् पुंस्) "the/a god" = deva- -- देव- (nominal stem) + -s -- -स् (case ending of the nominative singular)

In Sanskrit dictionaries and grammars, nouns are not cited in the nominative singular (as for example in Latin) but in the form of the nominal stem (in the case of multi-stemmed nouns: one of the nominal stems), e.g.

*   "deva m." -- "देव m." (masculine)
*   "vacana n." -- "वचन" n." (neuter)
*   "vyakti f." -- "व्यक्ति f." (feminine)

## 2.3. The Nominative Singular = prathamā ekavacanam = प्रथमा एकवचनम्

The nominative singular (prathamā ekavacana) ends in -s = -स् or is endingless.

### 2.3.1. Nominative Singular in -s

The following nominal stems that end in a vowel form the nominative singular in -s:

*   Masculines in -a: e.g. deva m. = देव "god, heavenly being" -- Nom. sg.: devas = देवस्
*   Masculines in -i: e.g. kavi m. = कवि "poet" -- Nom. sg.: kavis = कविस्
*   Masculines in -u: e.g. guru m. = गुरु "master, teacher" -- Nom. sg.: gurus = गुरुस्

## 2.4. Sandhi -- सन्धि

The final sound of a word in Sanskrit also depends on the initial sound of the following word. This phenomenon is called sandhi (m.) = सन्धि ("connection").

### 2.4.1. Sandhi of final -s

See also the overview:

> Payer, Alois <1944 - >: Sandhi of final -s. -- (Materials on Sanskrit). -- URL: [http://www.payer.de/sanskritmaterialien/ssandhi.htm](http://www.payer.de/sanskritmaterialien/ssandhi.htm)

Final -s

*   in absolute final position (end of sentence, end of verse) is replaced by Visarga (-ḥ):
    e.g.
    devas = देवस् » devaḥ = देवः
    kavis = कविस् » kaviḥ = कविः
    gurus = गुरुस् » guruḥ = गुरुः
    
*   before voiceless sounds:
    *   before voiceless gutturals (k, kh): is replaced by Visarga (-ḥ)
    *   before voiceless labials (p, ph): is replaced by Visarga (-ḥ)
    *   before voiceless sibilants (ś, ṣ, s): is replaced by Visarga (-ḥ)
        e.g.
        devas + śivaḥ » devaḥ śivaḥ = देवः शिवः "Śiva is a god"
        kavis + kālidāsas » kaviḥ kālidāsaḥ = कविः कालिदासः "Kālidāsa is a poet"
        
    *   before voiceless palatals (c, ch): is replaced by -ś
    *   before voiceless retroflexes (ṭ, ṭth): is replaced by -ṣ
    *   before voiceless dentals (t, th): remains -s
        (i.e., in these cases, the sibilant of the class of the initial sound stands)
        e.g.
        sādhus + caitanyas » sādhuś caitanyaḥ = साधुश्चैतन्यः "Caitanya is a saint"
        
*   before voiced sounds:
    *   after non-a-vowels (except before r-): is replaced by -r
        e.g.
        kavis + māghas » kavir māghaḥ = कविर्माघः "Māgha is a poet"
    *   after non-a-vowels before r-: -s is dropped, a short vowel preceding the -s is replaced by the corresponding long vowel, a preceding long vowel remains unchanged
        e.g.
        gurus + rāmas » gurū rāmaḥ = गुरू रामः "Rāma is a teacher"
        
    *   after a-: final -as
        *   before voiced consonants and before a: is replaced by -o, initial a- is dropped after this -o
            e.g.
            devas viṣṇus » devo viṣṇuḥ = देवो विष्णुः "Viṣṇu is a god"
            devas agnis » devo 'gniḥ = देवो ऽग्निः "Agni (fire) is a god"
        *   before vowels other than -a: -as is replaced by -a with hiatus
            e.g.
            devas + indras » deva indraḥ = देव इन्द्रः "Indra is a god"

## 2.5. Vocabulary

Learn the following words:

deva m. -- देव: heavenly being, god; prince, king (especially in address)

īśvara m. -- ईश्वर: lord, ruler, supreme deity, God (in the monotheistic sense)

brāhmaṇa m. -- ब्राह्मण: Brahmin, member of the first estate (varṇa m.), the spiritual estate—priestly estate—and teaching estate.

kṣatriya m. -- क्षत्रिय: Kṣatriya, member of the second estate (varṇa m.), the princely estate and warrior estate

vaiśya m. -- वैश्य: Vaiśya, member of the third estate (varṇa m.), the nourishing estate and merchant estate

śūdra m. -- शूद्र: Śūdra, member of the fourth estate (varṇa m.), the servant estate and service estate (craftsmen, etc.)

dvija m. -- द्विज: twice-born: member of one of the first three estates (varṇa m.); born twice: once natural birth, the second time through the ceremony of upanayana (n.), where the boy is initiated; in particular: dvija = Brahmin; dvija also = bird or another egg-born creature: they appear in a first birth as an egg, in a second when hatching.

varṇa m.-- वर्ण: color, estate (by birth)

Max Weber <1864 - 1920> defines "estate" like this:

> "Estate" shall be a plurality of people who, within an association, effectively
> 
> 1.  claim a special social esteem—possibly also
> 2.  special monopolies of estate.
> 
> Estates can arise
> 
> 1.  primarily, through their own estate lifestyle, among which, in particular, through the type of occupation (lifestyle or occupational estates),
> 2.  secondarily, hereditary-charismatic, through successful prestige claims by virtue of estate descent (birth estates),
> 3.  through estate appropriation of political or hierocratic powers as monopolies (political or hierocratic estates).
> 
>   
> The birth estate development is regularly a form of (hereditary) appropriation of privileges to an association or to qualified individuals. Any fixed appropriation of chances, in particular [of] powers [or earning] chances, tends to lead to estate formation. Any estate formation tends to lead to the monopolistic appropriation of powers and earning chances.
> 
> While income classes grow on the ground of the market-oriented economy, estates arise and exist preferably on the ground of the monopolistically liturgical or the feudal or the estate patrimonial provision of needs of associations.
> 
> A society shall be called "estate-based" if the social structure occurs preferably by estates, "class-based" if it occurs preferably by classes. From "classes", the "social" class is closest to "estate", the "income class" furthest. Estates are often formed according to their focus by property classes.
> 
> Every estate society is _conventional_, ordered by rules of conduct, therefore creates economically irrational consumption conditions and in this way prevents free market formation through monopolistic appropriations and through the elimination of the free disposal of one's own earning capacity."
>
