Max Weber <1864 - 1920> defines "estate" like this:

> "Estate" shall be a plurality of people who, within an association, effectively
> 
> 1.  claim a special social esteem—possibly also
> 2.  special monopolies of estate.
> 
> Estates can arise
> 
> 1.  primarily, through their own estate lifestyle, among which, in particular, through the type of occupation (lifestyle or occupational estates),
> 2.  secondarily, hereditary-charismatic, through successful prestige claims by virtue of estate descent (birth estates),
> 3.  through estate appropriation of political or hierocratic powers as monopolies (political or hierocratic estates).
> 
>   
> The birth estate development is regularly a form of (hereditary) appropriation of privileges to an association or to qualified individuals. Any fixed appropriation of chances, in particular [of] powers [or earning] chances, tends to lead to estate formation. Any estate formation tends to lead to the monopolistic appropriation of powers and earning chances.
> 
> While income classes grow on the ground of the market-oriented economy, estates arise and exist preferably on the ground of the monopolistically liturgical or the feudal or the estate patrimonial provision of needs of associations.
> 
> A society shall be called "estate-based" if the social structure occurs preferably by estates, "class-based" if it occurs preferably by classes. From "classes", the "social" class is closest to "estate", the "income class" furthest. Estates are often formed according to their focus by property classes.
> 
> Every estate society is _conventional_, ordered by rules of conduct, therefore creates economically irrational consumption conditions and in this way prevents free market formation through monopolistic appropriations and through the elimination of the free disposal of one's own earning capacity."
> 
> \[Weber, Max <1864 - 1920>: Economy and Society: An Outline of Interpretative Sociology. -- 5th, revised ed., student edition. -- Tübingen: Mohr, 1976. -- ISBN: 3-16-538521-1. -- P. 625 f.\]

## Lesson 11

iti इति : so, thus

(It stands after the formulation of a thought, wish, statement, or quote, serving as quotation marks ("). e.g. साधवः स्वर्गं गच्छन्तीति ब्राह्मणा वदन्ति "Brahmins say: 'Holy ones go to heaven'" = "Brahmins say that holy ones go to heaven". For translation, it should be noted that there is no indirect speech in Sanskrit; therefore, constructions with *iti* must often be rendered into English as indirect speech (anything else is poor translation!).

... (quote) ... iti śrutiḥ = "thus the Veda"

Often, a verb of thinking must be supplied after *iti*: "... iti he does such and such" = "thinking: '...' he does such and such" = "because ..., he does such and such". This is a way to express motives for actions. In English, such expressions must be rendered in idiomatic English and not in "Sanskritized" English (translations like "Thinking: 'I am hungry' he goes to the restaurant" are incorrect; correct: "Because he is hungry, he goes to the restaurant.")

evam एवम् : so, thus (adverbial, e.g., evaṃ jayati "thus he conquers". Note the difference in the use of *iti* and *evam*!)

na न : not

(Negates individual words or entire sentences. When negating individual words, *na* stands immediately before the negated word: na sādhuḥ "one who is not good". If a sentence is negated, *na* stands either at the beginning of the sentence – to emphasize a contrast or in emphatic negations – or immediately before the verb.)

asura m. असुर : demon

> "**ASURA**. 'Spiritual, divine.'
> 
> In the oldest parts of the Ṛgveda this term is used for the supreme spirit, and is the same as the Ahura of the Zoroastrians. In the sense of 'god' it was applied to several of the chief deities, as to Indra, Agni, and Varuṇa. It afterwards acquired an entirely opposite meaning, and came to signify, as now, a demon or enemy of the gods.
> 
> The word is found with this signification in the later parts of the Ṛgveda, particularly in the last book, and also in the Atharvaveda. The Brāhmaṇas attach the same meaning to it, and record many contests between the Asuras and the gods. According to the Taittirīya Brāhmaṇa, the breath _(asu)_ of Prajapati became alive, and "with that breath he created me Asuras." In another part of the same work it is said that Prajāpati "became pregnant. He created Asuras from his abdomen." The Śatapatha Brāhmaṇa accords with the former statement, and states that "he created Asuras from his lower breath." The Taittirīya Āraṇyaka represents that Prajāpati created gods, men, fathers, Gandharvas, and Apsarases from water, and that the Asuras, Rakṣasas, and piśācas sprang from the drops which were spilt. Manu's statement is that they were created by the Prajāpatis.
> 
> According to the Viṣṇu Purāṇa, they were produced from the groin of Brahma (prajāpati). The account of the Vāyu Purāṇa is: "Asuras were first produced as sons from his (Prajāpati's) groin. _Asu_ is declared by Brāhmaṇa to mean breath. From it these beings were produced; hence they are Asuras." The word has long been used as a general name for the enemies of the gods, including the Daityas and Danavas and other descendants of Kaśyapa, but not including the Rakṣasas descended from Pulastya.
> 
> In this sense a different derivation has been found for it: the source is no longer asu, \`breath,' but the initial _a_ is taken as the negative prefix, and _asura_ signifies 'not a god;' hence, according to some, arose the word _sura,_ commonly used for 'a god.'"
> 
> [Source: Dowson, John <1820-1881>: A classical dictionary of Hindu mythology and religion, geography, history, and literature. -- London, Trübner, 1879. -- s.v.]

## Lesson 21

*   **bhaj** 1 U (*bhajati*, Pass. *bhajyate*, PPP *bhakta*): to allot/bestow something to someone (Acc.); to love, honor, venerate.
    From this:
    *   **bhakti** f.: devotion, loyalty, love (in a religious context: love and respect for a personal god).
    *   **bhāga** m.: portion, part.
    *   **bhaga** m.: (good) portion, luck, well-being, dignity.
    *   **bhagavant** 3: possessing luck/dignity (epithet of Viṣṇu - Kṛṣṇa).

## Lesson 22

    *   **śakra** m.: "the Mighty One" (epithet of Indra).
    ![](/images/lekt2203.jpg)

*   **arha** 1 P (*arhati*, Pass. *arhyate*, PPP *arhita*, Inf. *arhitum*): to deserve (to be worthy of), to be allowed, to be obliged to, should. (In the 2nd person, *arha* + Infinitive is often used as a mild command: "You should...").

    *   **caraṇa** n., m.: foot.
    *   **carita** n.: life conduct, life deeds.
    *   **brahmacarya** n.: practice of the Veda (*brahman*) = study of the Veda in the first stage of life (that of the *brahmacārin*), which requires strict sexual abstinence; therefore also: celibate lifestyle.
