# Lesson 19

## 19.1. The Relative Clause

Schema:

Relative pronoun -- Relative clause, containing the noun to which the relative clause refers -- (Demonstrative pronoun) -- Main clause

The relative clause may also follow the main clause, but it cannot be embedded within the main clause.

The relative pronoun and the antecedent—which, in contrast to German, is situated within the relative clause—are in the case syntactically required by the relative clause. The demonstrative pronoun is in the case syntactically required by the main clause.

Examples:

Note: In content of a general nature, the optative is often used instead of the indicative. Consequently, many of our examples would preferably be rendered in the optative.

1. The agent (कर्तृ) of the sentence is further specified by a relative clause:

> यो नरः पुण्यं करोति स स्वर्गं गच्छति =  यो नरः पुन्यं करोति तेन स्वर्गं गम्यते = येन नरेण पुण्यम् क्रियते तेन स्वर्गं गम्यते u.s.w. = "A man who performs meritorious deeds goes to heaven."

2. The direct object (कर्म) of the main clause is further specified by the relative clause:

> यो ब्राह्मणो देवान्यजते तं देवा रक्षन्ति = "The gods protect a Brahmin who performs sacrifice to them."

3. The further specification of a noun from the main clause is contained within the relative clause:

> ये नराः पापं कुर्वन्ति तेषां पुत्रा धनं न लभन्ते = "The sons of men who do evil do not obtain wealth."

4. The relative pronoun and the antecedent are in the genitive (षष्ठी):

> यस्य नरस्य पुत्राः पापं कुर्वन्ति स न सुखवान् = "The man whose sons do evil is not happy."

5. The relative pronoun and the antecedent are in the instrumental (तृतीया):

> येन शत्रुणा ग्रामो जितस्तं स्विषन्ति = "They hate the enemy who has conquered their village."

6. The relative pronoun and the antecedent are in the accusative (द्वितीया):

> यं नरं देवी रक्षति स सुखमाप्नोति = "A man whom the goddess protects attains happiness."

## 19.3. Vocabulary

artha m.: purpose, goal, meaning (of a word), wealth, possession, assets. artham (acc.), arthena (inst.) with genitive or as the second member of a *tatpuruṣa*: for the sake of..., in order to.

artha is one of the three aims of life (*puruṣārtha*), as described in secular and religious literature:

> dharma m.: the attainment of merit through doing what is right, or at least the avoidance of evil that would result from non-compliance with *dharma*
> 
> artha m.: instrumental-rational behavior, the attainment of prosperity
> 
> kāma m.: sensual pleasure, especially in the sexual domain
