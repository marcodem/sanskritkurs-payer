# Lesson 23

## 23.1. The Infinitive (*tumun*)

In Sanskrit, if one wishes to express that an activity is performed for the sake of another activity ("in order to"), one can use the **Infinitive** (*tumun*) for the activity which is the aim. The Infinitive primarily designates purpose or intention:

> *rāmo guruvacanaṃ śrotuṃ gataḥ* = "Rāma went in order to hear the master's discourse."

Note that the Infinitive—with a few clearly defined exceptions—cannot function as the subject or object of a verb. For phrases like "He learns to dance," one must use a verbal noun instead of the Infinitive.

The Infinitive is also used with verbs and nouns meaning "to wish" or "to long for," provided the agent (*kartṛ*) of the Infinitive and the verb are identical:

> *rāmo guruvacanaṃ śrotumicchati* = "Rāma wishes to hear the master's discourse."

The Infinitive is also used with verbs meaning "to be able," "there is," "to know," "to begin":

> *sādhuradharmaṃ kartuṃ na śaknoti* = "A holy man cannot do unrighteousness."
> *asti bhoktumannam* = "There is food to eat."

The Infinitive is also used with words meaning "sufficient," "capable," and with nouns meaning "ability," "power," "skill":

> *astyagnevibhavaḥ sarvaṃ dagdhum* = "Fire has the power to burn everything."

It can also be used with words meaning "time" in expressions like "It is time to...":

> *kālo bhojanaṃ sevitum* = "It is time to devote oneself to food" = "It is time to eat."

The Infinitive can be used actively or passively. A passive infinitive in English is rendered in Sanskrit by putting the verb on which the infinitive depends (most commonly *śak* "to be able") into the passive.

Regarding the object, the Infinitive behaves like a verbal form: in an active construction, the direct object stands in the **Accusative** (*dvitīyā*); in a passive construction, the direct object stands in the **Nominative**.

e.g.,
*   *sādhuradharmaṃ kartuṃ na śaknoti* = *sādhunādharmo kartuṃ na śakyate* = "A holy man cannot do unrighteousness."

In the 2nd person, *arha* + Infinitive is often used as a mild command: "You should..."

When the Infinitive depends on a noun, it may not be joined with it into a compound, except for *Bahuvrīhis* whose second member is **kāma** or **manas**:
*   *vaktukāmaḥ* = *vaktuṃ kāmo yasya saḥ* = "one whose desire it is to speak; one who wishes to speak."

## 23.2. Formation of the Infinitive (*tumun*)

**Full grade root + -tum**
OR
**Full grade root + -i- + -tum**

Final consonants of the root are changed before *-tum* according to the same rules as before the *-ta* of the PPP. Verbs with preverbs form the infinitive in the same way as simple roots.

Examples:
*   *diś* → *deṣṭum*
*   *rud* → *roditum*

There are no fixed rules for the use of the connecting vowel *-i-*. Its distribution largely matches that of the Future tense.

Note the following infinitives for roots learned so far:
*   *gam* → *gantum*
*   *pracch* → *praṣṭum*

Roots with *-ra-* as the full grade of *-ṛ-*:
*   *dṛś* → *draṣṭum*
*   *sṛj* → *sraṣṭum*

Other roots with *-ṛ-* in the penultimate position optionally take *-ra-* or *-ar-* if they are *aniṭ*.
