# Lesson 11

## 11.1. Double Accusative

Some verbs can govern a double Accusative (dvitīyā):

Accusative of the person and Accusative of the thing.

These include:

*   Verbs of speaking: speaking to someone (Accusative) about something (Accusative); saying something (Accusative) to someone (Accusative).
    
*   Verbs of asking: asking someone (Accusative) about something (Accusative).
    
*   Verbs of teaching: teaching someone (Accusative) something (Accusative).
    
*   Some other verbs, which will be noted in the vocabulary lists.
    

If such a construction is put into the passive, the person (who is asked, etc.) is put into the Nominative (prathamā), while the thing (about which one is asked, etc.) remains in the Accusative.

e.g.

rāmo brāhmaṇaṃ dharmaṃ pṛcchati = रामो ब्राह्मणं धर्मं पृच्छति = "Rāma asks the Brahmin about the Dharma."

» Passive: rāmeṇa brāhmaṇo dharmaṃ pṛcchyate = रामेण ब्राह्मणो धर्मं पृच्छ्यते = "Rāma asks the Brahmin about the Dharma." (Literally: "The Brahmin is asked about the Dharma by Rāma.")

This rule only applies when both a person and a thing are objects. If a thing is the only object, it stands in the Nominative (prathamā) according to the basic schema of the passive sentence:

e.g.

rāmo dharmaṃ pṛcchati = रामो धर्मं पृच्छति = "Rāma asks about the Dharma."

» Passive: rāmeṇa dharmaḥ pṛcchyate = रामेण धर्मः पृच्छ्यते = "Rāma asks about the Dharma." (Literally: "The Dharma is asked about by Rāma.")

## 11.2. Vocabulary

iti इति : so, thus

(It stands after the formulation of a thought, wish, statement, or quote, serving as quotation marks ("). e.g. साधवः स्वर्गं गच्छन्तीति ब्राह्मणा वदन्ति "Brahmins say: 'Holy ones go to heaven'" = "Brahmins say that holy ones go to heaven". For translation, it should be noted that there is no indirect speech in Sanskrit; therefore, constructions with *iti* must often be rendered into English as indirect speech (anything else is poor translation!).

... (quote) ... iti śrutiḥ = "thus the Veda"

Often, a verb of thinking must be supplied after *iti*: "... iti he does such and such" = "thinking: '...' he does such and such" = "because ..., he does such and such". This is a way to express motives for actions. In English, such expressions must be rendered in idiomatic English and not in "Sanskritized" English (translations like "Thinking: 'I am hungry' he goes to the restaurant" are incorrect; correct: "Because he is hungry, he goes to the restaurant.")

evam एवम् : so, thus (adverbial, e.g., evaṃ jayati "thus he conquers". Note the difference in the use of *iti* and *evam*!)

na न : not

(Negates individual words or entire sentences. When negating individual words, *na* stands immediately before the negated word: na sādhuḥ "one who is not good". If a sentence is negated, *na* stands either at the beginning of the sentence – to emphasize a contrast or in emphatic negations – or immediately before the verb.)

putra m. पुत्र : son

(In India, it was necessary to beget a son who could perform the sacrifices for the ancestors.)

dharma m. धर्म : ("that which is firm," i.e.) right, law, custom, character

(Corresponds most closely to the medieval *lex naturae* = natural law, moral law, and proper social order. *Dharma* is not the same for everyone for Hindus; rather, everyone has their own specific duties according to *varṇa* and *āśrama* (stage of life; see Basham, Wonder p. 159f.): *svadharma*)

Opposite of dharma:

adharma m. अधर्म : injustice, unrighteousness

iṣ 6 P (icchati) इष् इच्छति : to wish, desire (iṣ-ccha-ti) (classified by indigenous grammarians in the 6th present class, although it is a formation with a present-stem suffix -ccha- like *gam ga-ccha-ti*)
