# Lesson 12

## 12.1. Perfect Passive Participle (PPP)

One method of forming passive sentences in the past tense is through the construction using the so-called Perfect Passive Participle (PPP).

In reality, the PPP is not a true participle, as it is not derived from a tense stem. Rather, it is a nominal formation from the root using the primary suffixes *-ta* or *-na*. For this reason, in Indian grammar, one refers to the suffix *kta*.

## 12.1.1. Schema for Transitive Verbs

Agent (kartṛ) in the Instrumental (tṛtīyā) -- direct object (karman) in the Nominative (prathamā) -- Perfect Passive Participle

In this case, the PPP agrees with the object in number, case, and gender.

An auxiliary verb ("to be") is not required.

e.g.,

sādhunā svarga āptaḥ = साधुना स्वर्ग आप्तः = "(By the virtuous, a heaven was attained) = The virtuous person has attained a heaven."

brāhmaṇena devīṣṭā = ब्राह्मणेन देवीष्टा = "(By the Brahmin, the goddess was worshipped with a sacrifice) = The Brahmin has worshipped the goddess with a sacrifice."

## 12.1.2. Schema I for Intransitive Verbs and Verbs of Motion

Agent (kartṛ) in the Nominative (prathamā) — Perfect Passive Participle

In this instance, the PPP agrees with the agent in number, case, and gender. For intransitive verbs (verbs without a direct object) and verbs of motion, the Perfect "Passive" Participle carries an active meaning.

e.g.

kṣatriyā nagaraṃ gatā = क्षत्रिया नगरं गता = "The Kṣatriya woman has gone into the city."

## 12.1.3. Schema II for Intransitive Verbs and Verbs of Motion

Agent (*kartṛ*) in the Instrumental (*tṛtīyā*) — PPP in the Nominative Singular Neuter

e.g.,

kṣatriyeṇa (nagaraṃ) gatam = क्षत्रियेण (नगरं) गतम् = "(By the Kṣatriya, (into the city) was gone) = The Kṣatriya has gone (into the city)."

The construction according to Schema II is much rarer than the construction according to Schema I.

## 12.2. On the Meaning of the PPP

While the so-called "Perfect Passive Participle" primarily carries a passive meaning for transitive verbs (āpta = "having been reached") and an active meaning for intransitive verbs and verbs of motion (gata = "gone"), there are certain verbs for which the PPP can possess both active and passive meanings:

e.g.,

*   gata = गत = "gone" (active); but also: gato mārgaḥ = गतो मार्गः = "a traveled path" (passive)
*   āpta = आप्त = "having been reached" (passive), "having reached" (active)

## 12.3. Formation of the PPP

The following formation methods occur (the respective PPP must be learned for every root!):

(mostly) athematic root

*   \+ -ta
    *   with connecting vowel -i- preceding: -ita
    *   without connecting vowel: -ta
*   \+ na

The feminine stems are: -tā, -itā, -nā; the neuter declines like phala n.

## 12.4. Laws of Sound Combination within Words

In *aniṭ* formations, the following laws of internal sound combination must be observed. These laws are of fundamental importance for the understanding of the entirety of Sanskrit morphology.

1. k, t, p before an unvoiced stop (e.g., t, th) remain unchanged:

> pt, tt, kt = प्त्, त्त्, क्त्

2. ct is replaced by kt = क्त्:

> muc + ta » muk-ta = मुक्त

3. št is replaced by ṣṭ = ष्ट्:

> dṛś + ti » dṛṣṭi = दृष्टि  
> dṛś + ta » dṛṣṭa =  दृष्ट  
> viś + ta » viṣṭa = विष्ट

4. A voiced unaspirated stop—excluding j—is replaced by its corresponding voiceless unaspirated stop when preceding a voiceless sound:

> d + t(h) » tt(h) = त्त्, त्थ् (crucial for the 2nd class of the present tense)

5. jt is replaced by kt or ṣṭ (not optional!):

> yuj + ta » yuk-ta = युक्त  
> yaj + ta » iṣ-ṭa =  इष्ट (« \*yj-ta)  
> sṛj + ta » sṛṣ-ṭa = सृष्ट  
> sṛj + ti » sṛṣ-ṭi = सृष्टि

6. Voiced aspirated stop + voiceless stop $\rightarrow$ voiced unaspirated stop + voiced aspirated stop (Bartholomaes' Law):

> bh-t » b-dh
> 
> > labh + ta » lab-dha = लब्ध
> 
> dh-t » d-dh
> 
> > budh + ta » bud-dha = बुद्ध  
> > yudh + ta » yud-dha = युद्ध  
> > krudh + ta » krud-dha = क्रुद्ध

7. h-t is replaced by ḍh, accompanied by the lengthening of a preceding i or u; or by gdh. Before such a ḍh, a is replaced by o, or more rarely by ā:

> dah + ta » dag-dha = दग्ध  
> guh + ta » gūḍha = गूढ (guh 1 (gūhati) "to hide something")  
> muh + ta » mug-dha / mūḍha = मुग्ध मूढ  
> sah + ta » soḍha = सोढ
