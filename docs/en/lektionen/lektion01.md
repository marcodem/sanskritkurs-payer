# Lesson 1

### गणेशपूजनम्  
gaṇeśapūjanam  
Veneration of Gaṇeśa

![](/images/lekt0102.jpg)  
Fig.: Gaṇeśa, Adamspeak, Sri Lanka  
[Image source: Wikipedia, public domain]

लम्बोदर नमस् तुभ्यं  
सततं मोदकप्रिय |  
निर्विघ्नं कुरु मे देव  
सर्वकार्येषु सर्वदा ||

lambodara namas tubhyaṃ  
satataṃ modakapriya |  
nirvighnaṃ kuru me deva  
sarvakāryeṣu sarvadā ||

O Pot-bellied one, obeisance to You,  
Ever fond of the Modaka sweets!  
O God, make all my undertakings  
Always free from obstacles!

### मङ्गलचरणम्  
maṅgalacaraṇam  
"Auspicious Beginning"

![](/images/lekt0101.jpg)  
Fig.: Ardhanarīśvara  
(Image source: Wikipedia, public domain)

वागर्थाविव संपृक्तौ  
वागर्थप्रतिपत्तये |  
जगतः पितरौ वन्दे  
पार्वतीपरमेश्वरौ ||

vāgarthāviva saṃpṛktau  
vāgarthapratipattaye |  
jagataḥ pitarau vande  
pārvatīparameśvarau ||

(Kālidāsa: Raghuvaṃśa 1.1)

I salute the parents of the world,  
Pārvatī and Śiva,  
Who are as firmly united  
As word and meaning for the understanding  
Of word and sense.

At the beginning, a curiosity:

> "When William Jones [1746–1794] and Henry Thomas Colebrooke (1765—1857) first thoroughly studied, partially translated, and found that Sanskrit had a rich literature and no small relationship with classical languages, they met with no small resistance. Since this intimate relationship of Sanskrit to the European languages so geographically distant could not be reconciled with the old views, which either derived all languages from Hebrew or largely isolated them from one another, the famous philologist Dugald Stewart (1753—1828) took the simplest way out by declaring the whole story of the Sanskrit language a lie. He wrote an essay in which he sought to prove that it had been forged by the rogue Brahmins after the pattern of Greek and Latin and that the language as well as the literature were a forgery. This view was extensively developed in 1840 by the professor in Dublin, Charles William Wall (Göttingische gelehrte Anzeigen 1842 p. 1888)."

## 1.2. The sounds of Sanskrit according to the traditional Indian classification and its scientific transcription

![](/images/lekt0106.jpg)

Modern Sanskrit dictionaries are arranged in the order of this classification. This classification and the order of the sounds is essential for understanding Sanskrit grammar and must therefore be known by heart:

> simple vowels (*samānākṣara* -- समानाक्षर):  
> अ a, आ ā, इ i, ई ī, उ u, ऊ ū,  ऋ ṛ, ॠ ṝ, ऌ ḷ
> 
> diphthongs (*sandhyakṣara* -- सन्ध्यक्षर):  
> ए e, ऐ ai, ओ o, औ au
> 
> consonants (*vyañjana* / *hal* -- व्यञ्जन / हल्):  
> क ka, ख kha, ग ga, घ gha, ङ ṅa  
> च ca, छ cha, ज ja, झ jha, ञ ña  
> ट ṭa, ठ ṭha,  ड ḍa, ढ ḍha, ण ṇa  
> त ta, थ tha, द da, ध dha, न na  
> प pa, फ pha, ब ba, भ bha, म ma  
> य ya, र ra,  ल la, व va  
> श śa, ष ṣa, स sa  
> ह ha

### 1.2.1. On the pronunciation of individual sounds

| Sound | Description |
| :--- | :--- |
| **a - अ** | "Short a" is often pronounced like ə by Indians—since ancient times. In Europe, it is pronounced as short a, in Bengal as short dark o. |
| **ā - आ** | pronounced as long a. |
| **i - इ** | pronounced as short i. |
| **ī - ई** | pronounced as long i. |
| **u - उ** | pronounced as short u. |
| **ū - ऊ** | pronounced as long u. |
| **ṛ - ऋ** | pronounced like ri (rarely like re; e.g., Kṛṣṇa = Krishna). |
| **ṝ - ॠ** | pronounced like long ri. |
| **ḷ - ऌ** | pronounced like li. |
| **e - ए** | pronounced like long e (= the e in German "Ehe"). |
| **ai - ऐ** | pronounced like the diphthong ai. |
| **o - ओ** | pronounced like long o (= the o in German "Oheim"). |
| **au - औ** | pronounced like the diphthong au. |
| **kh, gh, ch, jh, ṭh, ḍh, th, dh, ph, bh** | the h after these consonants means that they are to be spoken aspirated (i.e., with a clear follow-up breath). *th* is never to be spoken as in English "the" or "thin," but as t + h (e.g., as in English "hothouse"). *ph* is never like f, but like p + h (e.g., as in English "sheephouse"). |
| **ṅ, ñ, ṇ, n, m** | Nasals. |
| **ṭ, ṭh, ḍ, ḍh, ṇ** | Cerebral (retroflex) sounds: the tip of the tongue is turned up as far as possible towards the palate. |
| **ś - श** | Palatal s-sound: spoken with the tongue against the hard palate (similar to English "sh"). |
| **ṣ - ष** | Cerebral s-sound: spoken with the tip of the tongue turned up (similar to English "sh"). |
| **s - स** | dental s-sound (similar to the s in English "set"). |
| **ḥ** | Visarga -- विसर्ग: a final aspiration (breath) after a vowel. |
| **ṃ** | Anusvāra -- अनुस्वार: before sibilants, h, l: nasalization of the vowel; at the end of a word = m; within a word before consonants: the nasal corresponding to the following consonant: *saṃdhi* -- संधि = *sandhi* -- सन्धि. |

The best pronunciation is achieved by reading the sentences, verses, or words quite slowly and monotonically, with exact consideration of the length of the vowels.
