# Lesson 32

## 32.1. The Past Tenses

In older Sanskrit literature and according to traditional grammarians, the three past tenses are clearly distinguished in usage:

*   **The Aorist** (*luṅ, adyatanī*): denotes either the simple completion of an action or an event that occurred on the current day (the near past).
*   **The Imperfect** (*laṅ*): denotes events that occurred before the current day (the remote past).
*   **The Perfect** (*liṭ*): like the Imperfect, denotes the remote past, but is used for events that the speaker did not personally witness.

In classical Sanskrit literature, however, these three past tenses are generally used without any difference in meaning.

## 32.2. The Imperfect (*laṅ*)

**Formation:**
**Augment *a-* + Present Stem + Secondary Ending**

The three persons of the singular Parasmaipada Imperfect are formed from the **strong** present stem in athematic classes. All other forms are formed from the **weak** present stem. The Imperfect exists only in the Indicative.

Examples:
*   **bhū**: 3. sg. Impf. P. *abhavat* (*a-bhava-t*)
*   **su**: 
    - 3. sg. Impf. P. *asunot* (*a-suno-t*)
    - 3. pl. Impf. P. *asunvan* (*a + sunu + an*)

## 32.3. Rules for the Augment

1.  If the augment *a-* is prefixed to a root starting with a **vowel**, the augment and the initial vowel merge into the ***vṛddhi*** grade of the root vowel.
    - *iṣ* → *aicchat* (*a- + iccha-t*)
    - *i* → *ait* / *āyan*
    - *ās* → *āsta*

2.  If **preverbs** are present, the augment *a-* is placed between the preverb and the root.
    - *ā-gam* → *āgacchat* (*ā + a + gaccha-t*)
    - *upa-gam* → *upāgacchat* (*upa + a + gaccha-t*)

## 32.9. Translation Exercise

Once, an old man was walking between villages and became weary on the path. Therefore, he went to the foot of a mango tree standing nearby for a rest. In that tree, there were ripening fruits. A desire for them arose in the old man. But climbing the tree, he was not able to take them. By chance, in that tree, some monkeys were sitting, eating fruits. Looking at them, the old man became delighted. What did he do? He took some stones and, targeting the monkeys, threw them. The monkeys, becoming angry, plucked some fruits and threw them at the old man. The old man, with joy, took them and went to his desired destination. Oh, the cleverness of the old man! (from: *saṃskṛtabālādarśa*)
