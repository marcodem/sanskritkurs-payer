# Lesson 13

## 13.1. PPP on -na-

No fixed rules can be established for the distribution of PPPs using -ta- versus -na-. However:

Almost all roots ending in -d form the PPP using -na-. In this case, -d-n- is replaced by -n-n-, which otherwise does not happen in word sandhi but is an imitation of sentence sandhi.

e.g.

pad 4 Ā PPP: panna 3 = पन्न

## 13.2. Further notes on the use of the PPP

1\. The PPP of verbs meaning "to think," "to wish," "to know," "to understand," "to worship," as well as some other verbs marked with *ñi* in the Dhātupāṭha (the root list accompanying Pāṇini), has not only a past meaning but can also be used with a present meaning:

e.g.

> iṣṭa "desired / wished for" (i.e., not only desired in the past but also in the present)  
> tvarita "hurrying, hurried" (PPP of tvar 1Ā "to hurry"; Dhātupāṭha: ñitvárā)

2\. The PPP can be used attributively as an adjective:

e.g.

> iṣṭaṃ phalam "the desired fruit (e.g., of one's actions)"

In this case, if api follows the PPP, api has the meaning "although":

e.g.

> iṣṭam api phalaṃ na labhate = "Although he desires the fruit, he does not obtain it."

3\. The neuter singular of the PPP of any verb can also be used as a verbal abstract:

e.g.

> gata n.: "the going, the gait / way"  
> nṛtta n.: "the dancing, the dance"

## 13.3. Attributive Determination of Nouns (Word Order)

In prose, attributes (modifiers) stand before the word they define in the normal word order. Adjectival attributes agree with the noun in number, case, and gender:

e.g.

> sādhur iṣṭaṃ phalaṃ paśyati = "A holy man sees the desired fruit (of his works)."

## 13.4. Formation of Adjectives in -mant and -vant (taddhita)

Possessive adjectives are formed from nouns using the taddhita suffixes -mant or -vant. Their meaning is: "possessing that which is designated by the underlying noun."

-vant is attached to nouns whose last or second-to-last sound is a, ā, or m, as well as to nouns ending in a stop consonant; to other nouns, -mant is usually attached:

e.g.

> paśumant "possessing livestock"  
> guṇavant "possessing good qualities / virtue"

## 13.5. Stem Gradation of Nouns

Stems in -mant and -vant belong to the nominal stems with stem gradation.

In nominal stems with stem gradation, a distinction is made between strong and weak cases. In the strong cases, the stem-forming suffix – or, in the case of root nouns, the radical part – has full grade (Guṇa) or lengthened grade (Vṛddhi); in the weak cases, it has the zero grade (Tiefstufe).

Strong cases are:

As the first member of a compound, a noun with stem gradation stands in the weak stem (for three-stem nouns, in the so-called middle stem).

To understand the individual forms of the declension of nouns in -mant and -vant, the following rules of word sandhi are necessary:

## 13.6. On Word Sandhi

1\. Number of final consonants of a word: Of two or more consonants with which a word would end, only the first consonant is preserved; the others are dropped. The combination -r- + consonant is permitted.

2\. Voiceless stops are replaced by their corresponding voiced stops before voiced stops (but not before nasals):

e.g.

> -t + bh- » -d-bh- द्भ्  
> -k + bh- » -g-bh- ग्भ्  
> -c + bh- » -g-bh- ग्भ्  
> -c + dh- » -g-dh- ग्ध्

## 13.7. Case Endings of Consonantal Stems

For stems ending in a consonant:

*   The ending of the Accusative (dvitīyā) plural masculine and feminine is -as.
*   For regularly formed stems, the neuter is endingless in the Nominative (prathamā) and Accusative (dvitīyā) singular.
*   For regularly formed stems, the ending of the Nominative and Accusative plural in the neuter is -i.

## 13.9. Vocabulary

eva एव : emphasizes the preceding word; often corresponds to emphatic stress in spoken language. It often does not need to be translated but acts as a sort of "emoticon."

asura m. असुर : demon

> "**ASURA**. 'Spiritual, divine.'
> 
> In the oldest parts of the Ṛgveda this term is used for the supreme spirit, and is the same as the Ahura of the Zoroastrians. In the sense of 'god' it was applied to several of the chief deities, as to Indra, Agni, and Varuṇa. It afterwards acquired an entirely opposite meaning, and came to signify, as now, a demon or enemy of the gods.
> 
> The word is found with this signification in the later parts of the Ṛgveda, particularly in the last book, and also in the Atharvaveda. The Brāhmaṇas attach the same meaning to it, and record many contests between the Asuras and the gods. According to the Taittirīya Brāhmaṇa, the breath _(asu)_ of Prajapati became alive, and "with that breath he created me Asuras." In another part of the same work it is said that Prajāpati "became pregnant. He created Asuras from his abdomen." The Śatapatha Brāhmaṇa accords with the former statement, and states that "he created Asuras from his lower breath." The Taittirīya Āraṇyaka represents that Prajāpati created gods, men, fathers, Gandharvas, and Apsarases from water, and that the Asuras, Rakṣasas, and piśācas sprang from the drops which were spilt. Manu's statement is that they were created by the Prajāpatis.
> 
> According to the Viṣṇu Purāṇa, they were produced from the groin of Brahma (prajāpati). The account of the Vāyu Purāṇa is: "Asuras were first produced as sons from his (Prajāpati's) groin. _Asu_ is declared by Brāhmaṇa to mean breath. From it these beings were produced; hence they are Asuras." The word has long been used as a general name for the enemies of the gods, including the Daityas and Danavas and other descendants of Kaśyapa, but not including the Rakṣasas descended from Pulastya.
> 
> In this sense a different derivation has been found for it: the source is no longer asu, \`breath,' but the initial _a_ is taken as the negative prefix, and _asura_ signifies 'not a god;' hence, according to some, arose the word _sura,_ commonly used for 'a god.'"
> 
> [Source: Dowson, John <1820-1881>: A classical dictionary of Hindu mythology and religion, geography, history, and literature. -- London, Trübner, 1879. -- s.v.]
