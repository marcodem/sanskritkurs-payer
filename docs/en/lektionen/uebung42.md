# Exercise 42

Translate:

प्रकृत्यैव यः कर्माणि क्रियमाणानि पश्यति स आत्मानमकर्तरं पश्यति ॥१॥

> He who sees that actions are being performed by material nature alone, sees that the self is not the doer.

कृष्णस्तस्य लोकस्य पिता माता पितामहो धातास्ति ॥२॥

> Kṛṣṇa is the father, mother, grandfather, and creator of this world.

आचार्याः पितरः पुत्राश्च पितामहाः श्वशुरा नप्तरो युद्धायावस्थिताः । एतान्न हन्तुमिच्छामीत्यर्जुनो भगवद्गीतायामुवाच ॥३॥

> "My teachers, fathers, sons, grandfathers, fathers-in-law, and grandsons have positioned themselves for battle. I do not wish to kill them!"—thus said Arjuna in the Bhagavad Gītā.

कवयो लब्धपुत्रतायाः पितॄन्मातॄश्च तुष्टुवुः ॥४॥

> Poets praised fathers and mothers because they obtained sons.

भर्त्रा भार्या भर्तव्या । तस्माद्भार्येत्युच्यते ॥५॥

> The wife must be supported by the husband. Therefore she is called "she who is to be supported" (bhāryā).

सत्पुत्रः पितृभ्यः पिण्डान्ददाति । पितृभिः पिण्डदानमश्यत एवं च सुखजीवो जीवितुं शक्यते ॥६॥

> A good son gives rice-balls (piṇḍas) to the ancestors. The ancestors eat the offered rice-balls, and thus they can lead a happy life.

भ्रात्रा स्वसा न विवोड्धव्या । भातरि स्वसारं कामयमाने देवाः क्रुध्यन्ति ॥७॥

> A brother must not marry his sister. When a brother desires his sister, the gods are angry.

क्थं भर्तुर्भ्रातोच्यते । देवेति भर्तुर्भ्राता वक्तव्यः ॥८॥

> How is the brother of the husband called? The husband's brother should be called "brother-in-law" (deva).

नप्तॄणां लाभं पितैच्छत् ॥९॥

> The father desired to obtain grandsons.

Sītā's Marriage

In ancient times, there was a king named Janaka in Mithilā. He had a daughter named Sītā. She was incomparable in beauty and virtue. Many royal princes who wished to marry her sent ambassadors to Janaka.

Janaka, however, wished to give her to a royal prince who was full of manliness. Therefore, he determined that she must be won through manliness. Namely thus: he had in his possession an extremely heavy bow. Janaka promised that whoever lifted this bow and placed an arrow on it would marry his daughter.

When they heard this promise, royal princes came by the hundreds. But not a single one of them could even move the bow. It is known that even Rāvaṇa, the ruler of Laṅkā, came with pride and returned full of shame. When Janaka saw that all the royal princes were returning, he sank into deep worry about who would be the husband of his daughter. Meanwhile, Viśvāmitra brought Rāma, the son of Daśaratha, the ruler of Ayodhyā, there along with Lakṣmaṇa. At the word of the great Ṛṣi Viśvāmitra, the glorious Rāma lifted the bow as if in play. As soon as he aimed an arrow on it, the bow broke into two pieces.

"Good, good!" the people praised the manliness of the glorious Rāma.

King Janaka's heart became joyful. Then he had Daśaratha and the others come and had the wedding of Sītā and Rāma celebrated with great splendor.

(from: Saṃskṛtapratḥamādarśa)
